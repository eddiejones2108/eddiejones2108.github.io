Functional Flocks
Eddie Jones;

The ability of nature to organise itself is not just impressive but often beautiful too. When a group of starlings flock together, they create a mesmerising acrobatic performance. Apparently, we're not the only species that like to dance!

It's not just biologists that find the sight fascinating. There is a whole field of computer science dedicated to abstracting and replicating these emergent behaviours, particularly in optimisation. If you can't beat 'em, join 'em!

Boids was one of the first algorithms designed to mimic flocking behaviour. Each boid is governed by three simple forces:

  • Separation: try not to crash
  • Cohesion: follow the crowd
  • Alignment: look where the crowd is looking

While these forces update individual boids, they are inherently dependent on the position of neighbouring boids, let's call it the context…

Context-aware Pogramming

While monads have been widely adopted in the functional programming community, their dual, co-monads, are sadly not so popular 😞 I'm going to assume you've already come across the former as there are so many brilliant monad tutorials that my introduction would just confuse the matter.

Briefly though monads are just a convention for adding structure to the output of a function. This allows us to clearly differentiate between the result and its side-effects:

f :: a -> m b

The side-effects must be composable, and there is a notion of having no side-effects, i.e. being pure:

join :: m (m a) -> m a
return :: a -> m a

Similarly, comonads add structure to the input of the function; they tell us what's the context and what's the focus. How do we decide which bit of information belongs where? Well, this is no different to deciding what counts as a side-effect; perhaps I meant to put that bug there!?… it depends on the application.

f :: w a -> b

The corresponding notion of being pure is when the function doesn't depend on it's context. The extract function does exactly that, just returning the focus:

extract :: w a -> a

To understand the analogous notion of composition, it is best to think of our comonad as some sort of container. Within this container there are lots of objects of type a, one of which is the current focus. However, we could focus on any of them, and they will all have a different view of their context. The duplicate function records this context from each possible perspective, creating a container of containers:

duplicate :: w a -> w (w a)

The Flock Comonad

Our three boid update functions have a natural focus and context; the current boid we're updating and it's neighbours. In other words, the flock is a container full of boids that is ripe for comonadification.

type Flock a = R2 -> Maybe a

Concretely, a flock is represented by a partial function between positions and boids. The boid in focus will always be in position (0, 0). But it's in the duplicate function that the real magic happens:

As we just mentioned, each boid will have a different view of the flock, and the duplicate function encodes this view. In terms of linear algebra we can think of each boid being replaced by a copy of the flock except there has been a change of basis so that the focus, i.e. the zero point, is that boid.

We can now compose and extend our boid update functions so that they apply to the entire flock in a particularly clean way.

applyForce = extend cohesion . extend align . extend separation
